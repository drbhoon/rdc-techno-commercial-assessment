# RDC Sales Force Assessment Skill File — Technical Skill

## 1) Skill identity
- **Skill name:** RDC Techno-Commercial Assessment — Technical Skill
- **Population:** RDC sales force
- **Assessment mode:** **Voice-based**
- **Delivery mode:** Standalone assessment, administered separately from the selling-skill test
- **Question source file:** `rdc-Techno-commercial-technical - question bank.xlsx`
- **Source bank size:** **48 questions**
- **Questions to administer per attempt:** **20**
- **ERP/process questions:** **Not applicable; exclude such additions if introduced later**

## 2) Purpose
This assessment checks whether a sales professional has enough technical understanding of RMX to speak credibly with customers, handle common site complaints responsibly, and escalate correctly.

The candidate is **not** expected to behave like a laboratory specialist or design engineer. However, the candidate must know enough to:
- understand common concrete problems
- ask the right questions
- give safe first-level guidance
- avoid technically wrong commitments
- bring QC / technical team in at the right time

## 3) Core design principles
1. **Field practicality over textbook theory**
   - High score requires practical site relevance.
   - Purely academic answers should not score high.

2. **Safe first-level technical handling**
   - Candidate should know what can be advised immediately and what must be escalated.

3. **Complaint triage matters**
   - The candidate should identify likely causes, immediate risk, first checks, and escalation path.

4. **No unsafe improvisation**
   - Casual advice such as “add water” without controls should be penalized.

5. **Customer confidence with technical honesty**
   - Candidate should sound technically aware without pretending to know what must be tested or verified.

## 4) Competency framework
### T1. RMX Fundamentals & Product Understanding
Understanding of:
- quality consistency
- workability
- strength
- freshness
- customer-service relevance of RMX quality

### T2. Site Condition Diagnosis
Ability to connect technical complaints with site conditions such as:
- hot weather
- cold weather
- rapid drying
- poor site preparation
- delayed unloading
- pump issues
- transit delays

### T3. Complaint Handling for Fresh Concrete
Ability to respond to complaints like:
- low slump
- fast setting
- segregation
- bleeding
- pump blockage
- inconsistent workability

### T4. Complaint Handling for Hardened Concrete
Ability to respond to complaints like:
- cracks
- scaling
- dusting
- curling
- honeycombing
- discoloration
- permeability
- spalling
- exposed rebar
- damaged edges
- popouts

### T5. Corrective Action & Preventive Guidance
Ability to suggest practical first-level corrective actions and future preventive measures.

### T6. Technical Risk Judgment
Ability to avoid wrong advice, identify what needs verification, and know when QC/technical escalation is compulsory.

### T7. Communication & Escalation Discipline
Ability to explain issues calmly, document facts, gather site details, and involve the right technical authority.

## 6) Randomization and test assembly rules
Present **20 questions** per attempt.

### Mandatory distribution
Use this target mix:
- T1 RMX Fundamentals & Product Understanding — **3 questions**
- T2 Site Condition Diagnosis — **3 questions**
- T3 Complaint Handling for Fresh Concrete — **4 questions**
- T4 Complaint Handling for Hardened Concrete — **4 questions**
- T5 Corrective Action & Preventive Guidance — **3 questions**
- T6 Technical Risk Judgment — **2 questions**
- T7 Communication & Escalation Discipline — **1 question**

### Assembly rules
- Include at least:
  - **6 fresh concrete / active-pour questions**
  - **6 hardened concrete / post-pour questions**
  - **4 cause-diagnosis questions**
  - **4 response/escalation questions**
- Avoid stacking near-identical defect questions in the same attempt when better variety is available.

## 7) Voice response rules
- Preferred response length: **30 to 75 seconds**
- Candidate may answer in English, Hinglish, or Hindi with technical keywords
- Grammar should not be scored
- Technical meaning and practical judgment should drive scoring

## 8) Evaluation philosophy
A high-scoring technical response should typically cover:
1. likely cause(s)
2. important checks/questions
3. immediate safe action
4. what should not be done
5. when QC / technical escalation is needed
6. how to communicate with the customer

A weak answer usually:
- sounds theoretical and detached from site reality
- gives unsafe remedies
- skips fact-finding
- fails to distinguish between first advice and formal technical decision
- over-promises root cause without evidence

## 9) Scoring framework
Each answer is scored on a **1 to 5** scale.

### Sub-scores
#### A. Technical correctness — 35%
Is the response technically sound?

#### B. Practical site applicability — 25%
Would the advice help in a real site situation?

#### C. Diagnostic quality — 15%
Did the candidate identify plausible causes and relevant checks?

#### D. Safety / risk judgment — 15%
Did the candidate avoid unsafe or unauthorized advice?

#### E. Escalation & communication discipline — 10%
Did the candidate involve QC/technical support appropriately and communicate responsibly?

### Score anchors
#### Score 5 — Strong
- Technically correct
- Practical
- Balanced
- Safe
- Good escalation judgment

#### Score 4 — Good
- Mostly correct and practical
- Minor omissions
- Still reliable

#### Score 3 — Acceptable
- Basic understanding present
- But answer is generic, incomplete, or weak on root-cause thinking / escalation

#### Score 2 — Weak
- Important gaps
- Risk of wrong field guidance
- Poor structure

#### Score 1 — Poor
- Technically wrong, unsafe, or highly unrealistic

## 10) Mandatory downgrade rules
Cap the score at **3** if:
- answer is generic and not linked to RMX site reality
- candidate gives only theory, no practical next step
- candidate jumps to conclusion without asking basic checks
- candidate ignores the need for QC/technical escalation where appropriate

Cap the score at **2** if:
- candidate casually recommends adding water or other uncontrolled site alteration
- candidate ignores curing, unloading delay, weather, handling, or site practice where clearly relevant
- candidate gives technically incomplete guidance that can worsen the issue

Cap the score at **1** if:
- candidate recommends unsafe or clearly wrong action likely to damage quality or create structural risk

## 11) Positive markers
Reward answers that include:
- checking site conditions before blaming mix
- verifying unloading delay / transit time / weather / site handling
- separating immediate mitigation from long-term prevention
- involving QC/technical team when issue affects quality, strength, or durability
- preserving customer confidence without making unsupported technical claims

## 12) Red-flag indicators
Flag for manager review if the candidate repeatedly shows:
- weak understanding of slump/workability basics
- unsafe attitude toward water addition
- inability to distinguish fresh-concrete issues from hardened-concrete issues
- poor diagnosis of customer complaints
- failure to escalate serious technical matters

## 13) Transcript handling instructions
Normalize these common spoken forms:
- “slump low”, “low slump”, “workability low”
- “cube fail”, “strength fail”
- “setting fast”, “flash set”, “quick set”
- “bleeding”, “segregation”, “honeycomb”
- “pump choke”, “blockage”
- “cracks next day”, “plastic shrinkage”, “crazing”
- “bug holes”, “pits”, “pockmarks”
- “rebar visible”, “cover issue”, “exposed steel”

Do not penalize mixed-language responses if the technical meaning is clear.

## 14) Final output format for each question
For each response, produce:
- **Question ID**
- **Competency / competencies**
- **Score (1–5)**
- **Why this score**
- **What was good**
- **What was missing**
- **Manager coaching note**

## 15) Final test summary output
After all 20 questions, produce:
- overall score %
- competency-wise score
- top 3 strengths
- top 3 improvement areas
- red flags, if any
- readiness recommendation:
  - Technically ready for sales role
  - Ready with coaching
  - Borderline
  - Not ready

## 16) Practical interpretation guide
### Technically ready for sales role
Can handle routine customer technical discussions and first-level complaint handling correctly.

### Ready with coaching
Can handle basic matters but needs coaching on diagnosis, escalation, or preventive guidance.

### Borderline
Understands some terms, but practical technical handling is inconsistent.

### Not ready
Insufficient technical judgment for customer-facing RMX discussions.

## 17) Source-bank normalization notes
The source file is broadly useful, but the application should apply the following clean-up logic:
- normalize numbering, because the workbook has a minor formatting inconsistency around the mid-section
- preserve source traceability, but the spoken UI may present lightly polished phrasing
- avoid overloading one test attempt with too many similar defect questions
- maintain balance between fresh-concrete and hardened-concrete topics

## 18) Gold-standard response pattern
A strong answer typically sounds like:
> “First I identify the most likely causes based on site conditions, transit, handling, weather, and placement practice. Then I suggest the immediate safe action, explain what should be checked, and bring in QC or the technical team where needed. I avoid casual technical commitments without verification.”

This pattern should score well when adapted correctly to the specific complaint.

## 19) What this skill file is optimizing for
This test is designed to identify sales people who can:
- talk technically with credibility
- avoid wrong field advice
- handle common RMX complaints responsibly
- strengthen customer trust through sound first-level guidance
